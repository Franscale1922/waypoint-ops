# Home Services Franchises: The Most Overlooked Category

If you've been searching for a franchise in food, fitness, or retail, you've probably skipped right past **home services**. Most candidates do. That's the opportunity.

Home services franchises are boring on the surface. Parking lot maintenance. Holiday lighting. Kitchen cabinet upgrades. Grill cleaning. Nothing about that list is going to get your pulse up at a dinner party. But these businesses solve problems that never go away, serve clients who pay reliably, and often operate in markets with no meaningful competition.

Here's what the category looks like up close.

---

## Why Home Services Gets Overlooked — And Why That's a Mistake

Consumer-facing brands get all the attention during franchise searches. You recognize the name. You understand the product. You can picture yourself in the business.

Home services doesn't have that advantage. Most of these concepts are B2B-adjacent (serving property managers, general contractors, commercial landlords) or hyper-niche (holiday lighting, grill cleaning, pavement maintenance). They don't advertise on television. Their customer base doesn't need much convincing because the problem is already there.

That invisibility creates real competitive advantages:

- **No meaningful national competition in most niches.** Pavement maintenance is a $29 billion industry with no major franchised brand owning it. That's rare at this point in franchising.
- **Recurring demand built into the business model.** Parking lots need annual sealcoating. Gutters fill up every fall. Grills get dirty every summer. You're not hunting new customers from scratch each year.
- **Lower entry points than consumer brands.** Many home services franchises launch from a home office with a van and a crew. No showroom, no retail fit-out, no inventory sitting on shelves.

---

## Three Specific Models Worth Knowing

Understanding the category is one thing. Here are three concrete models that illustrate the range of what "home services franchise" actually means.

**Pavement maintenance (B2B-focused)**
Think parking lots, driveways, commercial properties. This isn't a guy-with-a-paintbrush operation. Franchised pavement maintenance models bring proprietary coatings, CRM systems, and a structured sales process to a fragmented trade. Recurring revenue is built in because lots need annual touch-ups. One brand in this space is operating in a $29B market with no major national competitors. That window won't stay open indefinitely.

The catch: crew management and night work. Most jobs happen overnight or on weekends when lots are empty. If you want a 9-to-5 business, this is not it.

**Holiday lighting (seasonal residential and commercial)**
This one has an interesting footnote. Connor Groce (whose Franchise Anonymous research informs this piece) actually owned a multi-service home franchise early in his career. Window cleaning, pressure washing, gutters. He says he hated three of those four services and loved one: the holiday lighting side. This category now exists as a standalone franchise.

The model works because the competition is almost entirely local operators with no systems, no recurring client follow-up, and no ability to scale. A franchised approach adds centralized marketing, structured hiring, and the option to add permanent outdoor lighting as a year-round revenue stream. Launch cost is low. No storefront required.

The limitation: the core business is concentrated in October through January. You need a plan for the other eight months.

**Niche home services (grill cleaning, lighting, specialty cleaning)**
A category-within-a-category that doesn't get discussed in most franchise searches. Mobile grill cleaning targets upper-income neighborhoods where the service is consistently in demand and almost no one is delivering it with systems behind it. Low overhead. No office. Gear fits in a trunk.

This model works as a standalone or as a bolt-on for someone already in home services with established client relationships. The seasonal risk (cold markets slow down in winter) is real but manageable.

## The Operator Profile That Succeeds Here

Home services rewards people who are good at managing crews and processes, not necessarily people who love the work itself. You don't need to know how to clean a grill or seal a parking lot. You need to hire people who do, keep them on schedule, and close the initial commercial account.

The candidates who struggle in this category tend to fall into two types:

1. People who underestimate the management complexity (this is a real business, not a side hustle)
2. People who can't tolerate hands-on involvement in the early phase

Most home services franchises are not semi-absentee plays at launch. Year one typically requires full engagement, market development, and crew-building. By year two or three, most operators are managing from a higher level.

---

## What the Numbers Look Like

Ranges vary considerably by model:

- **Initial investment:** $60,000–$200,000 depending on equipment requirements and territory size
- **Revenue by year two (strong performers):** $400K–$1M+ for B2B-focused models with commercial accounts
- **Margins:** Higher than most consumer-facing service businesses, often 40–55% gross
- **Staffing:** Typically 2–10 crew members depending on model and scale

The pavement maintenance niche specifically has documented strong performers clearing $750K+ by year three. For a franchise with under $150K to get started, that's a return profile worth paying attention to.

---

## Bottom Line

Home services franchises don't get exciting headlines, and that's exactly why there's room to build something real. Competition is light. Demand is recurring. Entry costs are reasonable.

The question worth asking is whether your strengths line up with what these businesses actually need: crew management, local relationship-building, and operational discipline.
